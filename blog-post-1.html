The Fascinating History of Kona Coffee

Home › Blog
By Mele AkanaJune 12, 20256 min read

Seeds on a Volcanic Slope

The story of Kona coffee begins in 1828 when the Reverend Samuel Ruggles brought coffee plants to the Big Island from Brazil. He planted them in the rich volcanic soil of the Kona coast — a strip of land on the western slopes of Hualalai and Mauna Loa that turned out to be ideal coffee-growing territory.

The elevation (between 1,000 and 2,500 feet), the cloud cover that rolls in each afternoon providing shade, the well-drained volcanic soil, and the mild temperatures created what coffee scientists call a "microclimate" of exceptional quality. This combination is found in very few places on Earth.

The Kona Belt Today

The Kona coffee belt is roughly 30 miles long and 2 miles wide. Within this narrow strip, about 700 farms — most of them small family operations — grow what has become one of the world's most coveted specialty coffees. Production is limited; only about 2–3 million pounds of Kona coffee are produced annually, making it among the rarest commercial coffees in the world.

What Makes It Taste Different

Kona coffee is known for a smooth, mild flavor with low acidity, light body, and bright, clean notes. Depending on the farm elevation and processing method, you'll find notes ranging from chocolate and brown sugar to tropical fruit and macadamia nut. The volcanic soil contributes mineral complexity that you simply can't replicate elsewhere.

The 10% Problem

Hawaii law allows products to be labeled "Kona blend" with as little as 10% actual Kona coffee — the remaining 90% can be cheaper imported beans. This has been a persistent source of frustration for real Kona farmers. At Lehua Bake House, we serve only 100% Kona — sourced from farms we can name and visit. Always ask whether you're drinking 100% Kona or a blend.

Our Coffee Story

Our coffee director Mele grew up on her family's farm in Holualoa, just above Kealakekua Bay. Every bean we serve comes from farms within 20 miles — some from family members. When you drink a cup of Lehua Kona coffee, you're drinking something with real roots in this volcanic soil and a family's multi-generation story.
